# Web Analytics Analysis: The Traffic-Conversion Disconnect

## 1. Dataset Overview

This dataset contains **1,095 days** of daily web analytics data spanning **January 1, 2022 through December 30, 2024** (exactly 3 years, no gaps, no missing values). The seven columns are:

| Metric | Type | Range | Mean | Description |
|--------|------|-------|------|-------------|
| pageviews | int | 100–1,864 | 940 | Total daily page views |
| unique_visitors | int | 120–1,257 | 610 | Unique daily visitors |
| bounce_rate | float | 0.17–0.70 | 0.448 | Fraction of single-page sessions |
| avg_session_duration_sec | int | 73–298 | 180 | Average session length in seconds |
| new_signups | int | 5–27 | 14.8 | New user registrations per day |
| support_tickets | int | 0–10 | 3.0 | Support tickets filed per day |

## 2. Key Findings

### Finding 1: Traffic has grown 144% but signups are completely flat

This is the central finding of the analysis. Over three years, daily unique visitors nearly doubled from ~420/day to ~806/day (see `plots/01_time_series_overview.png`, Panel A in `plots/12_summary_dashboard.png`). Yet daily signups remained locked at approximately **15/day** across all three years:

| Year | Avg Visitors/Day | Avg Signups/Day | Total Signups | Signup Rate |
|------|-----------------|-----------------|---------------|-------------|
| 2022 | 420 | 14.9 | 5,444 | 3.55% |
| 2023 | 608 | 14.6 | 5,328 | 2.40% |
| 2024 | 806 | 15.0 | 5,475 | 1.86% |

Monthly signups have a coefficient of variation of only 5% (mean 451, std 21). The trend slope across months is +0.1 signups/month (p = 0.803, not significant). The conversion rate has declined by **64%** — from ~4.6% to ~1.7% — entirely because the denominator grew while the numerator stayed constant (see `plots/05_rates_over_time.png`, `plots/07_monthly_trends.png`).

**Evidence that signups are independent of traffic volume**: When visitors are binned into deciles, average signups are 14.3–15.4 across ALL deciles (from 208 to 1,053 visitors/day). A Poisson regression of signups on visitors yields a coefficient of -6.9e-06 with p = 0.829 — effectively zero. The partial correlation between signup rate and visitors, controlling for time, is r = -0.60 (p < 0.001), confirming this is not a spurious time-trend artifact (see `plots/06_traffic_vs_outcomes.png`).

### Finding 2: Signups follow a Poisson process with constant rate

The distribution of daily signups is remarkably well-described by a Poisson distribution with **λ = 14.84**. The dispersion ratio (variance/mean) is **1.03** — almost exactly the 1.0 expected from a Poisson process. The autocorrelation function shows no temporal structure at any lag — signups on one day are independent of signups on any other day (see `plots/09_autocorrelation.png`).

Similarly, support tickets follow a Poisson distribution with **λ = 2.96** (dispersion ratio = 0.98). CUSUM analysis shows no changepoints in the signup rate over the entire period (see `plots/11_signup_cusum.png`).

These findings indicate that signups and tickets are generated by a **fixed-rate random process** that is completely decoupled from traffic volume, engagement quality, and time.

### Finding 3: Traffic exhibits strong dual seasonality

STL decomposition (`plots/03_stl_decomposition.png`) reveals two layers of predictable variation in pageviews:

**Weekly seasonality**: Traffic peaks on Mondays (1,124 avg pageviews) and troughs on Thursdays (748), a swing of ~376 pageviews (Kruskal-Wallis H = 147, p < 0.001). Day-of-week effects relative to Monday:

| Day | Effect |
|-----|--------|
| Tuesday | -93 |
| Wednesday | -280 |
| Thursday | -376 |
| Friday | -332 |
| Saturday | -178 |
| Sunday | -24 |

**Annual seasonality**: Traffic follows a sinusoidal pattern with amplitude ~399 pageviews, peaking in spring (March-April) and troughing in late summer (August-September). This pattern repeats consistently across all three years (see monthly averages in `plots/07_monthly_trends.png`).

**Underlying trend**: After removing seasonality, the trend component shows a steady linear increase of **0.81 pageviews/day** (from ~556 to ~1,355 over 3 years). Linear growth fits slightly better than exponential (R² = 0.25 vs 0.22 for raw trend regression).

Critically, **neither weekly nor annual seasonality exists in any other metric**. Kruskal-Wallis tests for day-of-week effects on bounce rate (p = 0.34), session duration (p = 0.78), signups (p = 0.38), and tickets (p = 0.75) are all non-significant (see `plots/04_day_of_week_patterns.png`).

### Finding 4: Engagement metrics are stationary noise with no predictive power

Bounce rate and session duration are both:
- **Stationary**: No trend over 3 years, no seasonality, no autocorrelation
- **Normally distributed**: Bounce rate ~ N(0.448, 0.078), session duration ~ N(180, 40)
- **Uncorrelated with each other**: r = 0.001, p = 0.96
- **Uncorrelated with every other metric**: All pairwise correlations are between -0.03 and +0.04 (see `plots/02_correlation_matrix.png`, `plots/08_engagement_vs_outcomes.png`)

Signups are identical across bounce rate quartiles (14.8–15.0) and session duration quartiles (14.7–15.1). Even a two-way analysis crossing bounce rate and duration quartiles shows no interaction — signups range from 14.2 to 15.6 with no systematic pattern.

### Finding 5: A simple model explains 96% of pageview variance

A linear regression with trend (day number), weekly (day-of-week dummies), and annual (Fourier terms) components achieves **R² = 0.955** with MAE = 61 pageviews (~6.5% of the mean). Residuals show no systematic patterns (see `plots/10_pageview_model.png`).

In contrast, **no model can predict signups**. The null model (predict the mean of 14.84) achieves MAE = 3.1. A linear model with all available features achieves worse cross-validated performance (MAE = 5.83). A Random Forest achieves negative R². The best predictor of tomorrow's signups is simply "about 15."

### Finding 6: Very few anomalies, consistent with random variation

Only **3 days** out of 1,095 had pageview residuals beyond 3 standard deviations. Only **5 days** had signup counts anomalous under the Poisson model (p < 0.01), and only **4 days** for tickets. These counts are entirely consistent with expected false-positive rates from random variation.

## 3. Interpretation and Practical Implications

### The data tells a clear story: traffic growth is "empty"

The site has successfully grown traffic by 144% over three years, but this growth has produced **zero incremental signups**. The ~15 daily signups appear to come from a stable core audience that would visit and convert regardless of overall traffic levels. The additional visitors brought in by growth efforts (SEO, content marketing, paid acquisition, or organic spread) browse at the same bounce rate and session duration but do not convert.

### What this means for strategy

1. **Traffic volume is a vanity metric for this site.** Doubling visitors has not moved the signup needle. Resources spent on traffic acquisition have not generated proportional business value.

2. **The signup funnel has a fixed ceiling.** At 15 signups/day (Poisson-distributed), the addressable audience that the current product/positioning can capture appears to be saturated. Breaking through this ceiling likely requires changes to the product, pricing, or value proposition — not more traffic.

3. **Support scales sub-linearly with traffic.** Tickets (~3/day) are also independent of traffic volume, suggesting that support load is driven by the same fixed user base, not by total visitors.

4. **The weekly traffic pattern (Monday peak, Thursday trough) suggests a B2B or professional audience** that is most active early in the work week.

5. **The annual seasonality** (spring peak, late-summer trough) could reflect fiscal-year planning cycles, academic calendars, or industry-specific demand patterns.

## 4. Limitations and Self-Critique

### Assumptions that could be wrong

- **Independence assumption**: I assumed each day's signups are independent draws from a Poisson process. In reality, there could be cohort effects (e.g., a user who visits Monday might sign up Wednesday) that are invisible at the daily aggregate level. Individual-level data would be needed to test this.

- **Causal interpretation**: The finding that signups are independent of traffic volume does not prove that traffic *cannot* drive signups. It proves that the *kind* of traffic growth observed here did not. A qualitatively different traffic source (e.g., high-intent referrals vs. organic search) might behave differently.

- **Aggregate-level ecological fallacy**: Bounce rate and session duration are site-wide averages. It's possible that among the subset of visitors who convert, there *is* a strong engagement signal — but it's diluted by the growing mass of non-converting visitors.

### What I did not investigate

- **Channel-level breakdown**: The dataset lacks traffic source information. Understanding whether specific channels drive disproportionate signups would be the highest-value next step.
- **Funnel analysis**: We don't know where in the signup flow visitors drop off, or whether the signup flow itself changed during this period.
- **User-level data**: Session-level or user-level data would allow testing whether individual engagement metrics predict individual conversion.
- **External events**: Product changes, marketing campaigns, pricing changes, or competitive moves could explain the flat signup rate but are not captured in this data.

### What could be noise vs. signal

The near-perfect Poisson fit of signups (dispersion ratio 1.03) and the complete absence of any predictor with meaningful explanatory power raise the possibility that this dataset was generated from independent random processes rather than reflecting real user behavior. In a real web analytics setting, one would expect at least weak correlations between engagement and conversion. The total independence of all non-traffic metrics is unusual and worth investigating with additional data sources.
